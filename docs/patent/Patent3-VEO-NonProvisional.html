Background of the Invention

1. The Problem with Disposable Randomness

[0006] Conventional random number generators — including hardware RNGs, pseudo-random number generators (PRNGs), cryptographically secure PRNGs (CSPRNGs), and randomness APIs — produce numerical values as their output. Once generated, these values carry no metadata about their origin, generation process, source quality, or integrity guarantees. The generation process is discarded. The consumer receives a number and must trust the generator implicitly.

[0007] This disposable model is increasingly insufficient for modern applications where random or non-deterministic decisions have significant consequences:

[0008] Autonomous AI Systems. AI agents make decisions involving non-determinism — tool selection, response sampling, task routing, resource allocation, model selection, and autonomous execution approval. These decisions cannot be audited for fairness because the non-deterministic inputs carry no provenance. As AI agents gain authority over financial, medical, legal, and operational domains, the inability to verify their non-deterministic inputs creates regulatory, liability, and trust gaps.

[0009] Regulated Gaming. Casino, lottery, and online gambling systems are required by regulators (GLI-19, GLI-33, BMM, eCOGRA) to prove that random outcomes were generated fairly. Current certification processes test the RNG implementation but rely on audit logs produced by the same system being audited — creating a circular trust dependency that does not satisfy the principle of independent verification.

[0010] Decentralized Systems. Blockchain protocols, DAO governance mechanisms, fair-launch token distributions, and multi-party computations require randomness that is verifiably uncontrollable by any single party, including the generator. Existing on-chain randomness solutions (e.g., Chainlink VRF) provide derivation proofs but not quality scoring, multi-source provenance, or offline verification.

[0011] Scientific Computing. Monte Carlo simulations, stochastic modeling, and experimental protocols require reproducible, auditable entropy. Published simulation results should be verifiable by reviewers who can independently confirm the entropy provenance and quality used in the computation.

[0012] Enterprise Compliance. Enterprises deploying AI systems face growing regulatory requirements (EU AI Act, NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001, SOX) that demand auditability and transparency for algorithmic decisions. There is no standardized mechanism for attesting to the quality and provenance of non-deterministic inputs to these decisions.

2. Limitations of Existing Approaches

[0013] Verifiable Random Functions (VRFs). VRFs (e.g., U.S. Patent No. 12,401,532 B2 — Chainlink) prove that a specific output was correctly derived from a specific input and secret key. However, VRFs: (a) prove derivation, not entropy quality; (b) use a single entropy source; (c) do not score confidence; (d) do not carry source provenance; (e) require on-chain callbacks for verification; (f) do not support offline or air-gapped verification; and (g) do not produce self-describing, portable objects.

[0014] Quantum Random Number Generators (QRNGs). QRNGs produce high-quality entropy but return raw values without structured provenance, quality scoring, or independent verification capability. The consumer must trust the QRNG provider.

[0015] Public Randomness Beacons (e.g., drand, NIST Beacon). Beacons produce publicly verifiable random values but are single-source systems that do not aggregate multiple independent sources, do not score entropy quality, and do not produce self-describing verifiable objects.

[0016] Audit Logs. Audit logs record events but are written by the system being audited. A compromised or adversarial operator can modify, omit, or fabricate log entries. Logs prove assertion, not trust.

[0017] JSON Web Tokens (JWTs). JWTs provide portable, signed identity claims. However, JWTs do not address entropy quality, source provenance, multi-dimensional scoring, or the specific requirements of non-deterministic computation attestation. JWTs serve as an architectural analogy — the present invention does for uncertainty what JWT does for identity.

[0018] There exists a need for a system that transforms entropy from a disposable numerical output into a structured, self-describing, multi-source, quality-scored, cryptographically signed, optionally blockchain-anchored, independently verifiable trust artifact — an object that constitutes a verifiable trust assertion regarding the non-deterministic computation that produced it.

Summary of the Invention

[0019] The present invention provides a method and system for establishing portable, verifiable trust over non-deterministic computation through the generation, quality assessment, cryptographic attestation, optional immutable anchoring, and independent verification of structured trust artifacts called Verifiable Entropy Objects (VEOs).

[0020] A VEO is not merely a random number with metadata. It is a cryptographically verifiable trust assertion — a statement that says: "This output was generated using these sources, at this quality level, with this cryptographic attestation, under this policy, at this trust level." The assertion is independently verifiable by any party, without trusting the generator, without network access, and at any future time.

[0021] The invention provides:

  1. A Verifiable Entropy Object (VEO) format — a structured digital object encapsulating entropy payload, source provenance records, aggregation metadata, quality scoring, cryptographic proof, optional immutable ledger anchoring, and lineage references. The VEO is analogous to: PDF for documents, JWT for identity, and VEO for uncertainty.
  2. An Entropy Confidence Score (ECS) system — a multi-dimensional, weighted quantitative assessment of entropy quality and trust level, producing a score from 0 to 1000 across six or more independently computed dimensions with defined grade boundaries, configurable dimension weights, and fallback penalty rules.
  3. A canonical signing protocol — a deterministic method for transforming a VEO into a signing payload through deep key sorting, field inclusion/exclusion rules, and stable serialization, enabling asymmetric cryptographic signatures verifiable by any party through public key verification or address recovery.
  4. A multi-level verification framework — a trust state machine that assigns each VEO to one of a plurality of trust states based on progressively evaluated verification criteria, ranging from structural validity to full blockchain-anchored verification.
  5. A consumer policy system — configurable policy presets and custom policies specifying minimum trust requirements, with honest failure when requirements cannot be met.
  6. An entropy provenance graph — a lineage mechanism creating directed acyclic graphs of trust artifact derivation, enabling full audit trail construction and trust chain traversal.
  7. A decision attestation mechanism — linking VEOs to specific decisions or actions, enabling independent verification that a particular decision was derived from specific, scored, attested entropy.
  8. Entropy-as-a-Service architecture — network-accessible services for VEO generation, issuance, verification, scoring, and anchoring.

Detailed Description of Preferred Embodiments

[0022] The following description sets forth specific embodiments illustrative of the invention. These embodiments are not limiting; the invention encompasses all equivalents, variations, and modifications within the scope of the appended claims.

1. Verifiable Entropy Object (VEO) — Trust Artifact Format

1.1 Definition and Conceptual Framework

[0023] A Verifiable Entropy Object (VEO) is defined as a cryptographically verifiable trust assertion regarding a non-deterministic computation. The VEO encapsulates the entropy payload, source provenance, quality metrics, cryptographic attestation, optional immutable anchoring, and lineage references — all in a single, self-describing, portable digital object.

[0024] The conceptual progression is as follows: (a) entropy is the raw material — the non-deterministic value from one or more sources; (b) an uncertainty artifact is the structured container — entropy packaged with provenance and metadata; (c) a trust artifact is the signed, scored, verifiable container — the uncertainty artifact with cryptographic attestation and quality scoring; (d) a trust assertion is the statement the trust artifact makes — "this non-deterministic output was generated from these sources, at this quality, with this attestation, and can be independently verified." The VEO embodies all four levels.

[0025] The analogy is structural: Adobe PDF made documents portable and trusted; JWT made identity claims portable and trusted; VEO makes uncertainty portable and trusted. A VEO can be generated in one system, transmitted to another, verified by a third, and audited by a fourth — without any of these parties trusting each other. They need only trust the mathematics.

1.2 Object Classification System

[0026] The invention defines a classification system for trust artifacts based on their provenance characteristics:

ClassNameDescription
VEO-1ARaw SourceSingle-source entropy (one beacon round, one block hash, one QRNG output)
VEO-1BCompositeMulti-source aggregated entropy from two or more independent sources
VEO-1CAnchoredComposite entropy with immutable ledger anchor (blockchain, transparency log)
VEO-1DDecisionEntropy linked to a specific decision, action, or computation outcome

[0027] Class identifiers are protocol constants. VEO-1C is assigned only when a real immutable ledger anchor exists and has been confirmed. Policy requirements alone do not qualify an object for VEO-1C classification. VEO-1D is reserved for integration with decision attestation systems (see Section 10).

1.3 Required Fields

[0028] Each VEO contains the following required fields:

FieldTypeDescription
standardstringProtocol identifier (e.g., "VEO-1")
versionstringProtocol version (e.g., "1.0")
object_idstringUnique identifier (format: "veo_" + 32 hex characters)
object_classstringOne of: VEO-1A, VEO-1B, VEO-1C, VEO-1D
entropystringHex-encoded entropy payload ("0x...")
entropy_hashstringCryptographic hash of entropy ("0x...")
issued_atstringISO 8601 generation timestamp
providerstringProvider identifier
sourcesarrayArray of entropy source records (minimum 1)
proofobjectCryptographic proof/signature package
confidenceobjectEntropy Confidence Score (ECS) object

[0029] Optional fields include: expiration timestamp, aggregation metadata, immutable ledger anchor package, lineage references, and consumer policy applied. Required fields are immutable within a protocol version. New optional fields may be added in minor versions without breaking backward compatibility.

1.4 Entropy Source Records

[0030] Each entry in the sources array contains: a source identifier (e.g., "drand-mainnet"), a source type classification (e.g., "distributed_beacon", "blockchain", "hardware_rng", "qrng"), a source reference (e.g., "drand-round-29830144", "block-955153"), a fetch timestamp, an individual source entropy hash, and an optional source-provided signature. The source record provides complete provenance for each entropy contribution.

1.5 Multi-Source Aggregation

[0031] When multiple sources are used, the system aggregates entropy deterministically: source entropy values are normalized (lowercase), concatenated with a delimiter in a defined order, and processed through one or more cryptographic hash functions (SHA-256 in the preferred embodiment; SHA-3, BLAKE2, BLAKE3, or other cryptographic hash functions in alternative embodiments). The aggregation record preserves the method identifier, input ordering, and resulting hash, enabling any verifier to reproduce the aggregation from the individual source records.

2. Entropy Confidence Score (ECS) — Trust Quantification

2.1 Purpose

[0032] The Entropy Confidence Score (ECS) quantifies entropy quality — and by extension, the trust level of the non-deterministic output — as a single integer score from 0 to 1000. The ECS enables consumers to programmatically evaluate whether a trust artifact meets their requirements without inspecting individual sources. The ECS functions both as an entropy quality metric and as a trust quantification for non-deterministic outputs, broadening its applicability beyond randomness to any computation involving uncertainty.

2.2 Dimensions

[0033] The ECS is computed from six independently calculated dimensions, each producing a sub-score from 0 to 1000:

DimensionDefault WeightMeasurement
Freshness20%Time since generation; decays linearly over a configurable window
Diversity15%Ratio of unique live source identifiers to target count
Independence20%Ratio of unique source type categories to target count
Manipulation Resistance20%Difficulty of controlling aggregated output; increases with source count
Verification Success15%Rate of successful source verification at generation time
Availability10%Source availability at generation time

[0034] The composite score is: score = clamp(Σ(dimension_i × weight_i)), where clamp(n) = max(0, min(1000, round(n))).

[0035] In alternative embodiments, the dimensions and weighting coefficients may be modified based on application requirements. Embodiments include: dynamic weighting (adjusting weights based on real-time source conditions), policy-based weighting (consumer-specified weight profiles), industry-specific weighting (gaming regulators requiring higher manipulation resistance weight), machine-learned weighting (optimizing weights based on historical trust outcomes), and regulator-defined weighting (weights mandated by regulatory frameworks).

2.3 Grade Boundaries

[0036] Grade boundaries map the 0–1000 score to human-readable grades:

Score RangeGradeRecommended Use
900–1000AAACasino, regulatory, enterprise critical
800–899AAAI agents, gaming, finance
700–799ASimulation, general production
600–699BDegraded sources, non-critical
500–599CDevelopment, testing
0–499LOWDevelopment only

2.4 Fallback Penalty System

[0037] When a source adapter fails to retrieve live external entropy and falls back to local cryptographic randomness, defined penalties are applied to affected dimensions. For one fallback source: verification success is reduced by 100 points and manipulation resistance by 50. For two fallbacks: verification success reduced by 250, manipulation resistance by 150, diversity by 150. When all sources fall back: all penalties applied, total score capped at 650, grade cannot exceed B. The confidence metadata explicitly reports fallback count, live source count, and overall source status, ensuring the score never misrepresents entropy quality.

3. Canonical Signing Protocol

3.1 Canonicalization

[0038] Before signing, a VEO is transformed into a deterministic canonical form: (a) all object keys at every nesting level are sorted alphabetically (deep sort); (b) undefined values are removed; (c) null values are preserved explicitly; (d) stable serialization is applied to produce a deterministic byte string. The same VEO must always produce the same canonical byte string, regardless of the order in which fields were originally set.

3.2 Field Inclusion and Exclusion

[0039] The canonical signing payload includes: standard, version, object_id, object_class, entropy, entropy_hash, issued_at, expires_at, provider, sources, aggregation, confidence, lineage, and policy. Excluded: all fields within the proof object (the signature cannot include itself) and all fields within the anchor object (transaction hash, block number, and timestamp are only known after anchoring). The anchor field is set to null in the canonical payload.

3.3 Signing Process

[0040] In the preferred embodiment, the canonical payload is hashed using SHA-256, and the hash is signed using EIP-191 personal message signing with the provider's secp256k1 private key. The provider's address is recoverable from the signature, enabling verification without access to the private key. In alternative embodiments, any asymmetric cryptographic signature scheme capable of proving authenticity and permitting verification may be used, including but not limited to: Ed25519, RSA, ECDSA with other curves, post-quantum signature schemes (e.g., CRYSTALS-Dilithium, FALCON, SPHINCS+), or future asymmetric signature systems.

4. Multi-Level Verification Framework — Trust State Machine

[0041] The verification framework assigns each VEO to one of a plurality of trust states based on progressively evaluated verification criteria. The framework functions as a trust state machine where a VEO transitions between states as verification checks succeed or fail:

Trust StateRequirements
structurally_valid_unsignedSchema valid; entropy hash matches payload; no signature required
cryptographically_verifiedAll above + provider signature verified against public key / recovered address
anchored_verifiedAll above + immutable ledger anchor verified (Merkle proof or transaction hash)
policy_failedObject is structurally and cryptographically valid but does not meet the consumer's policy requirements (e.g., ECS below threshold, insufficient sources)
invalidHash mismatch, signature invalid, schema violation, or structural error

[0042] In alternative embodiments, additional trust states may include: pending (anchor submitted but not confirmed), partially_verified (some but not all verification checks passed), expired (VEO has passed its expiration timestamp), revoked (provider has issued a revocation), and superseded (a newer VEO has replaced this one). The state machine is extensible without protocol version changes.

[0043] Verification requires no cooperation from the provider. Given only the VEO object and the provider's public key (or Ethereum address), any party — anywhere, at any time, with no network access — can verify integrity (recompute entropy hash), authenticity (verify signature), quality (reproduce ECS from source records), and temporal anchoring (validate Merkle proof against on-chain root). This independent verification property is what distinguishes a trust assertion from an audit log.

5. Consumer Policy System

[0044] The system supports consumer-specified policies defining minimum trust requirements. Policies may specify: minimum ECS score, minimum number of entropy sources, required source types, anchor required (boolean), maximum latency, maximum age, allowed source types (inclusion list), and excluded source types (exclusion list). When a generated VEO does not meet the consumer's policy, the system returns the VEO with a policy_failed verification state and a structured explanation of which requirements were not met. The system does not silently substitute a lower-quality VEO.

6. Entropy Provenance Graph (Lineage)

[0045] Each VEO may reference parent VEOs through a lineage field containing an array of parent object identifiers and a lineage hash. This creates a directed acyclic graph (DAG) of trust artifact derivation. Use cases include: multi-stage entropy derivation (a composite VEO references raw single-source VEOs), decision chains (a VEO used for an agent decision links to the parent VEO that allocated the task), and audit trails (an auditor traverses the lineage graph from any leaf VEO to the root entropy sources).

[0046] The lineage mechanism enforces a trust non-amplification invariant: a child VEO's ECS cannot exceed its parent's ECS. Trust can degrade through the lineage graph (fewer sources, more processing) but cannot amplify.

7. Immutable Ledger Anchoring

[0047] A VEO may optionally be anchored to an immutable verification substrate. In the preferred embodiment, anchoring is performed via a Merkle batch system: multiple VEOs are organized as leaves of a Merkle tree, the root is recorded in a blockchain smart contract (Polygon, in the preferred embodiment), and each VEO carries a Merkle proof linking it to the on-chain root. In alternative embodiments, the immutable substrate may be any public blockchain, consortium chain, private chain, append-only distributed ledger, certificate transparency log, notarization network, or distributed timestamping system.

[0048] Anchoring provides temporal proof (the VEO existed at or before the block timestamp) and immutability (the anchor cannot be retroactively altered). Anchoring is asynchronous and does not block VEO issuance — tokens may be signed and served before anchoring completes.

8. Interoperability and Vendor-Neutral Verification

[0049] A critical design property of the VEO format is vendor-neutral verification. A VEO generated by Provider A can be verified by Provider B, consumed by Consumer C, and audited by Auditor D — without any of these parties trusting each other or having a business relationship. The verification requires only the VEO object and the provider's public key material.

[0050] This interoperability is achieved through: (a) the use of open, documented cryptographic standards (SHA-256, secp256k1, EIP-191 — or alternatives); (b) deterministic canonicalization ensuring that any implementation produces the same signing payload from the same VEO; (c) self-describing objects that carry all information needed for verification; and (d) public key infrastructure that enables independent signature verification.

9. Entropy-as-a-Service Architecture

[0051] In an embodiment for network-accessible deployment, the system provides the following services through RESTful APIs or equivalent network interfaces:

  1. Entropy Generation Service: Generates raw entropy from configured sources.
  2. VEO Issuance Service: Constructs, scores, signs, and returns VEOs with configurable policies.
  3. VEO Verification Service: Accepts VEOs and returns structured verification results with trust state assignments.
  4. ECS Scoring Service: Accepts source records and returns computed ECS scores.
  5. Anchoring Service: Manages batch anchoring of VEOs to immutable ledgers.

10. Decision Attestation — Linking Trust Artifacts to Decisions

[0052] In an embodiment for AI agent systems and autonomous decision-making, the system links VEOs to specific decisions. An autonomous software agent generates or references a VEO prior to making a non-deterministic decision and records the VEO identifier alongside the decision outcome. This creates a verifiable linkage between the entropy used and the decision made.

[0053] Decision attestation records may include: a decision identifier, a decision type (e.g., "tool_selection", "task_routing", "resource_allocation"), the selected option, a confidence measure, the VEO identifier, and an optional signature. A third party may reconstruct the decision context by verifying the associated VEO and related decision records.

[0054] Domains for decision attestation include: AI agent tool selection and task routing, multi-agent arbitration and coordination, autonomous execution approval, model selection and hyperparameter sampling, governance votes (DAO, corporate), clinical trial randomization, financial algorithm execution, and automated compliance decisions.

[0055] This embodiment reserves continuation opportunities for a future Verifiable Decision Object (VDO) patent, wherein the decision object references the VEO, the model, the inputs, and the policy, and the entire chain is independently verifiable.

Abstract of the Disclosure

A method and system for establishing portable, verifiable trust over non-deterministic computation through the generation, quality assessment, cryptographic attestation, optional immutable anchoring, and independent verification of structured trust artifacts called Verifiable Entropy Objects (VEOs). A VEO encapsulates an entropy payload, source provenance records, a multi-dimensional quality score (Entropy Confidence Score, 0–1000 across six dimensions), a cryptographic signature computed over a deterministic canonical form, optional immutable ledger anchoring, and lineage references. The VEO constitutes a cryptographically verifiable trust assertion: "This non-deterministic output was generated from these sources, at this quality, with this attestation, and can be independently verified." Any party can verify a VEO using only the object and the provider's public key — offline, air-gapped, and at any future time — without trusting the provider. The system supports object classification (single-source, composite, anchored, decision), a multi-level trust state machine for progressive verification, consumer policy enforcement, entropy provenance graphs for audit trail construction, decision attestation for AI agent systems, vendor-neutral cross-provider verification, and Entropy-as-a-Service deployment. The invention provides the Trust Evidence layer (Layer 3) of a five-layer Computational Trust Infrastructure, doing for uncertainty what JWT does for identity and PDF does for documents: making it portable, auditable, and trustworthy.
